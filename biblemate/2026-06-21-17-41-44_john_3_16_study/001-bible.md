# Step 001: Bible Translation Comparison (John 3:16)

**Persona**: Bible Textual Critic & Translation Specialist

This step presents the central text of John 3:16 in five prominent English translations, highlighting the diverse lexical and syntactic choices made by various translation committees (representing formal equivalence, dynamic equivalence, and optimal equivalence philosophies).

## Scripture Text Retrieval

- **[NET]** For this is the way God loved the world: He gave his one and only Son, so that everyone who believes in him will not perish but have eternal life.
- **[KJV]** For God so loved the world, that he gave his only begotten Son, that whosoever believeth in him should not perish, but have everlasting life.
- **[ASV]** For God so loved the world, that he gave his only begotten Son, that whosoever believeth on him should not perish, but have eternal life.
- **[ESV2016]** “For God so loved the world, that he gave his only Son, that whoever believes in him should not perish but have eternal life.
- **[NIV]** For God so loved the world that he gave his one and only Son, that whoever believes in him shall not perish but have eternal life.

## Technical Translation Analysis

### 1. The Rendering of *Οὕτως* (Houtos)
- **"God so loved"** (KJV, ASV, ESV, NIV): This traditional rendering of the adverb *οὕτως* (houtos) can be misunderstood in modern English as indicating mere *intensity* or *degree* ("how much" God loved).
- **"For this is the way God loved"** (NET): The NET Bible translation highlights that *οὕτως* is fundamentally an adverb of *manner* ("in this manner" or "this is how"). It demonstrates the *way* in which God’s love was manifested—namely, through the historic action of giving His Son.

### 2. The Translation of *μονογενῆ* (Monogenes)
- **"only begotten Son"** (KJV, ASV): Relies on the historical translation traditions influenced by the Latin *unigenitus*. It suggests a metaphysical relationship of eternal generation.
- **"one and only Son"** (NET, NIV) & **"only Son"** (ESV): Reflects modern lexicographical consensus. The term *μονογενής* is derived from *μoνoς* (sole/only) and *γένος* (class, kind, or family status), meaning "one-of-a-kind," "unique," or "supremely precious." Isaac is called Abraham's *μονογενής* in Hebrews 11:17 even though Abraham had other sons (Ishmael), because Isaac was the unique covenantal son.

### 3. Universal Conditional Phrase *πᾶς ὁ πιστεύων* (Pas ho pisteuon)
- **"whosoever believeth"** (KJV, ASV): Classic English, emphasizing the universal invitation.
- **"everyone who believes"** / **"whoever believes"** (NET, ESV, NIV): Modern gender-inclusive equivalents of the active participle *πιστεύων* with the universal adjective *πᾶς* ("all/everyone"). It shows that the invitation knows no national, social, or moral boundaries—salvation is contingent on personal active faith in Christ.

### 4. Contrast: Perish (*ἀπόληται*) vs. Life (*ζωὴν αἰώνιον*)
- **"shall not perish but have..."**: All five major translations are remarkably consistent in translating the subjunctive verb *ἀπόληται* (from *ἀπόλλυμι*, to destroy/lose) as "perish," contrasting it with having *ζωὴν αἰώνιον*.
- **"everlasting life"** (KJV) vs. **"eternal life"** (ASV, NET, ESV, NIV): "Everlasting" emphasis is strictly quantitative (duration), whereas "eternal" (from *αἰώνιος*) captures both the quantitative aspect (timeless, unending) and the *qualitative* aspect (sharing in the divine, spiritual life of God Himself, starting in the present).
